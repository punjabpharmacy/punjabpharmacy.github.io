## Section 1: Pharmacology & Clinical Pharmacy

### Q1. A patient with a history of deep vein thrombosis (DVT) is prescribed oral Warfarin. Which specific laboratory test is strictly mandated to monitor the therapeutic efficacy and safety of Warfarin therapy?
A) Activated Partial Thromboplastin Time (aPTT)
B) Prothrombin Time (PT) and INR
C) Bleeding Time (BT)
D) Complete Blood Count (CBC)

**Correct Answer:** B) Prothrombin Time (PT) and INR
**Explanation:** Warfarin is an oral anticoagulant that works by inhibiting Vitamin K-dependent clotting factors (II, VII, IX, X). Its effect is monitored using the Prothrombin Time (PT), which is standardized globally using the International Normalized Ratio (INR). A normal INR is 1.0, but for a patient on Warfarin, the target therapeutic range is typically strictly maintained between 2.0 and 3.0. Conversely, Heparin therapy is monitored using the aPTT.

### Q2. Tetracycline antibiotics (such as Doxycycline and Minocycline) exert their broad-spectrum bacteriostatic effect by binding specifically to which cellular structure in bacteria?
A) 50S ribosomal subunit
B) 30S ribosomal subunit
C) DNA Gyrase
D) Peptidoglycan cell wall

**Correct Answer:** B) 30S ribosomal subunit
**Explanation:** Tetracyclines halt bacterial protein synthesis by entering the bacterial cell and binding reversibly to the 30S ribosomal subunit. This binding physically blocks the attachment of aminoacyl-tRNA to the mRNA-ribosome complex. Without the addition of new amino acids, the growing peptide chain is halted, preventing bacterial replication. Macrolides (like Erythromycin) and Chloramphenicol act on the 50S subunit.

### Q3. Most pharmaceutical drugs are eliminated from the body via First-Order kinetics. However, Phenytoin, Aspirin, and Alcohol are clinically notorious for following Zero-Order elimination kinetics at high doses. What does "Zero-Order kinetics" mean?
A) A constant *fraction* of the drug is eliminated per unit of time
B) The drug is not eliminated and accumulates indefinitely
C) A constant *amount* of the drug is eliminated per unit of time
D) The elimination rate increases exponentially with the dose

**Correct Answer:** C) A constant *amount* of the drug is eliminated per unit of time
**Explanation:** In Zero-Order kinetics (also known as capacity-limited or saturable elimination), the metabolic enzymes in the liver become completely saturated. Because the enzymes are working at absolute maximum capacity, only a constant *amount* (e.g., 10 mg per hour) of the drug can be eliminated, regardless of how high the concentration is in the blood. This makes these drugs highly prone to sudden toxicity if the dose is slightly increased. 

### Q4. Nitrates, such as Sublingual Nitroglycerin, are the primary agents used for the acute relief of angina pectoris. At the cellular level, these drugs induce profound vasodilation by releasing Nitric Oxide, which directly stimulates the production of:
A) Cyclic AMP (cAMP)
B) Cyclic GMP (cGMP)
C) Adenosine triphosphate (ATP)
D) Phospholipase C

**Correct Answer:** B) Cyclic GMP (cGMP)
**Explanation:** Nitrates are prodrugs that denitrate inside vascular smooth muscle cells to release free Nitric Oxide (NO). Nitric Oxide then directly activates the intracellular enzyme soluble guanylyl cyclase, which drastically increases the synthesis of cyclic GMP (cGMP). Elevated cGMP levels lead to the dephosphorylation of myosin light chains, resulting in the relaxation of the smooth muscle and widespread vasodilation (primarily venous pooling, which reduces preload on the heart).

### Q5. Ondansetron is a highly effective, widely prescribed antiemetic drug, particularly valuable for preventing chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting (CINV). What is its exact pharmacological classification?
A) Dopamine D2 receptor antagonist
B) Histamine H1 receptor antagonist
C) Serotonin 5-HT3 receptor antagonist
D) Muscarinic M1 receptor antagonist

**Correct Answer:** C) Serotonin 5-HT3 receptor antagonist
**Explanation:** Ondansetron (brand name Zofran) revolutionized oncology care. It acts as a highly selective antagonist at the Serotonin 5-HT3 receptors. These specific receptors are located peripherally on vagal nerve terminals in the gastrointestinal tract and centrally in the Chemoreceptor Trigger Zone (CTZ) of the medulla oblongata. By blocking serotonin from binding to these receptors, Ondansetron effectively prevents the vomiting reflex triggered by highly emetogenic chemotherapy drugs.

### Q6. Cromolyn sodium is an inhaled medication utilized exclusively for the prophylactic management of bronchial asthma. Which of the following best describes its mechanism of action?
A) It causes direct relaxation of bronchial smooth muscle
B) It stabilizes the membranes of Mast cells, preventing degranulation
C) It blocks histamine receptors in the lungs
D) It inhibits the synthesis of leukotrienes

**Correct Answer:** B) It stabilizes the membranes of Mast cells, preventing degranulation
**Explanation:** Cromolyn sodium (and Nedocromil) are classified as Mast Cell Stabilizers. They possess zero bronchodilatory activity and are completely useless during an acute asthma attack. Instead, they act prophylactically by altering the function of delayed chloride channels in mast cell membranes. This prevents the influx of calcium, thereby totally inhibiting the degranulation and release of inflammatory mediators (like histamine and leukotrienes) when the patient is exposed to an allergen.

### Q7. Cyclophosphamide is a potent alkylating antineoplastic agent used for various cancers. A severe, unique dose-limiting adverse effect of this drug is Hemorrhagic Cystitis. Which specific compound causes this toxicity, and which drug is administered to prevent it?
A) Caused by Uric Acid; Prevented by Allopurinol
B) Caused by NAPQI; Prevented by N-acetylcysteine
C) Caused by Acrolein; Prevented by MESNA
D) Caused by Cyanide; Prevented by Sodium thiosulfate

**Correct Answer:** C) Caused by Acrolein; Prevented by MESNA
**Explanation:** Cyclophosphamide is a prodrug metabolized in the liver. One of its toxic metabolic byproducts is a highly reactive aldehyde called Acrolein. As Acrolein is excreted through the kidneys, it accumulates in the bladder and aggressively attacks the mucosal lining, causing severe bleeding (Hemorrhagic Cystitis). To prevent this, massive intravenous hydration and a sulfhydryl compound called MESNA (Mercaptoethane sulfonate) are administered. MESNA specifically binds to and neutralizes Acrolein in the bladder.

### Q8. Both Barbiturates (like Phenobarbital) and Benzodiazepines (like Diazepam) enhance the inhibitory neurotransmitter GABA. However, they do so via different mechanisms at the GABA-A receptor. Barbiturates specifically act by:
A) Increasing the *frequency* of chloride channel openings
B) Increasing the *duration* of chloride channel openings
C) Directly blocking sodium channels
D) Acting as a competitive antagonist at glutamate receptors

**Correct Answer:** B) Increasing the *duration* of chloride channel openings
**Explanation:** This is a classic board exam distinction. Both drug classes bind to allosteric sites on the GABA-A receptor complex to enhance the influx of negatively charged Chloride (Cl-) ions, hyperpolarizing the neuron. Benzodiazepines increase the *frequency* with which the chloride channels open. Barbiturates, however, increase the *duration* that the channels remain open. At high doses, barbiturates can mimic GABA completely, making them significantly more dangerous and prone to fatal respiratory depression in an overdose.

### Q9. In pharmacokinetics, the "Apparent Volume of Distribution" (Vd) is a theoretical parameter. A drug with an extremely high Volume of Distribution (e.g., Chloroquine, Vd > 10,000 L) indicates that the drug is:
A) Heavily bound to plasma proteins and remains in the bloodstream
B) Rapidly excreted by the kidneys within hours
C) Extensively distributed and accumulated in peripheral tissues (like muscle and fat)
D) Unable to cross the blood-brain barrier

**Correct Answer:** C) Extensively distributed and accumulated in peripheral tissues (like muscle and fat)
**Explanation:** The Volume of Distribution (Vd) relates the total amount of drug in the body to the concentration of the drug measured in the blood plasma. If a drug has a massive Vd, it means very little of it remains in the plasma; the vast majority has diffused out of the vascular compartment and sequestered deeply into peripheral tissues, lipids, and organs. Such drugs cannot be effectively removed from the body via hemodialysis during an overdose.

### Q10. Praziquantel is a highly effective, broad-spectrum anthelmintic medication. It is universally recognized as the absolute drug of choice for the treatment of infections caused by:
A) Nematodes (Roundworms)
B) Trematodes (Flukes) and Cestodes (Tapeworms)
C) Plasmodium species (Malaria)
D) Dermatophytes (Fungi)

**Correct Answer:** B) Trematodes (Flukes) and Cestodes (Tapeworms)
**Explanation:** Praziquantel is the definitive first-line treatment for Schistosomiasis (blood flukes) and infections caused by various tapeworms (like Taenia solium). It works by inducing a massive influx of calcium ions into the parasite's tegument. This causes rapid, violent tetanic contraction and vacuolization of the parasite's surface, leading to paralysis and exposing the parasite to destruction by the host's immune system. Roundworms are typically treated with Albendazole or Mebendazole.

## Section 2: Pharmaceutics & Engineering

### Q11. According to the strict quality control parameters laid out in the Indian Pharmacopoeia (I.P.), what is the maximum acceptable disintegration time for an uncoated, standard Hard Gelatin Capsule?
A) 15 minutes
B) 30 minutes
C) 45 minutes
D) 60 minutes

**Correct Answer:** B) 30 minutes
**Explanation:** Disintegration testing ensures that the dosage form breaks down into smaller particles, allowing the drug to dissolve. For standard, uncoated Hard Gelatin Capsules, the Indian Pharmacopoeia mandates that they must completely disintegrate in the specified medium (usually water or simulated gastric fluid at 37°C) within exactly 30 minutes. If the capsule shell does not dissolve and release its contents within this time limit, the batch fails QC. Uncoated tablets have a 15-minute limit.

### Q12. In the manufacturing of compressed tablets, excipients known as "Glidants" are added to the formulation. Their specific, primary pharmaceutical function is to:
A) Provide physical bulk to the tablet
B) Prevent the tablet from sticking to the punches
C) Improve the flowability of the powder or granules from the hopper to the die cavity
D) Promote the rapid breakdown of the tablet in the stomach

**Correct Answer:** C) Improve the flowability of the powder or granules from the hopper to the die cavity
**Explanation:** Glidants (such as Colloidal Silicon Dioxide, Talc, and Cornstarch) are critical for high-speed tableting. They act by lodging themselves into the microscopic irregularities of the granules, effectively acting like tiny ball bearings. This drastically reduces interparticulate friction, allowing the powder to flow smoothly and uniformly from the hopper into the die. This guarantees that every tablet has a uniform and accurate weight. 

### Q13. In physical pharmacy, coarse suspensions are categorized as either Flocculated or Deflocculated. Which of the following physical characteristics is true regarding a Flocculated suspension?
A) The particles settle extremely slowly over months
B) It forms a hard, non-dispersible cake at the bottom of the bottle
C) The sediment forms rapidly but is loosely packed and easily redispersible upon shaking
D) The supernatant liquid remains persistently cloudy after settling

**Correct Answer:** C) The sediment forms rapidly but is loosely packed and easily redispersible upon shaking
**Explanation:** In a flocculated suspension, the particles are intentionally engineered (using flocculating agents like electrolytes) to form loose, fluffy agglomerates called "flocs." Because these flocs are large and heavy, they settle very quickly. However, because they are loosely packed in a scaffolding-like structure, they cannot compress into a hard cake. A simple shake of the bottle instantly completely redisperses the drug, making it the ideal formulation for pharmaceutical suspensions.

### Q14. During the quality control testing of pharmaceutical Aerosols (like MDIs), the "Water Bath Test" is legally mandated and routinely performed on the production line to check for:
A) The particle size distribution of the spray
B) The exact moisture content inside the canister
C) Leakage from the canister seal or valve
D) The flammability of the propellant

**Correct Answer:** C) Leakage from the canister seal or valve
**Explanation:** Aerosol canisters are heavily pressurized. A microscopic defect in the crimping seal or the metered valve will cause the propellant to slowly leak out, resulting in a dead inhaler. To prevent this, every single finished aerosol canister is passed through a hot water bath (usually around 55°C). The heat increases the internal pressure; if there is any defect, distinct bubbles will escape through the water, allowing QC inspectors to immediately reject the faulty canister.

### Q15. In sterile manufacturing, membrane filters are utilized for cold sterilization. Before and after filtering a batch of sensitive injectable fluid, the physical integrity of the 0.22 µm membrane filter must be non-destructively verified using the:
A) Bubble point test
B) Pyrogen test
C) Sterility test
D) Karl Fischer test

**Correct Answer:** A) Bubble point test
**Explanation:** The Bubble Point Test is the universally accepted, non-destructive physical method used to confirm that a sterilizing membrane filter is completely intact and free from microscopic tears or defects. The filter is wetted, and gas pressure is slowly applied to one side. The exact pressure required to force the first continuous stream of bubbles through the largest pore of the wetted filter is recorded. If it bubbles at a pressure lower than the manufacturer's specification, the filter is torn, and the sterilization is invalid.

### Q16. Suppositories can be formulated using various bases. A major disadvantage of using a Glycerinated Gelatin base compared to Cocoa Butter is that Glycerinated Gelatin bases absolutely require the addition of a:
A) Hardening agent
B) Coloring agent
C) Preservative
D) Flavoring agent

**Correct Answer:** C) Preservative
**Explanation:** Glycerinated gelatin bases are frequently used for vaginal suppositories (pessaries) because they dissolve slowly in mucous secretions rather than melting at body temperature. However, because gelatin is an animal protein and the formulation contains water, it is highly prone to massive bacterial and fungal growth. Therefore, antimicrobial preservatives (like methylparaben or propylparaben) are strictly mandated. Cocoa butter is an anhydrous fat and generally does not require preservatives.

## Section 3: Pharmacognosy

### Q17. The Van Urk’s reagent is a classic, highly specific qualitative chemical test utilized in the pharmacognosy laboratory to identify the presence of:
A) Tropane alkaloids in Belladonna
B) Ergot alkaloids in Claviceps purpurea
C) Cardiac glycosides in Digitalis
D) Anthraquinone glycosides in Senna

**Correct Answer:** B) Ergot alkaloids in Claviceps purpurea
**Explanation:** The Van Urk’s test (also known as the p-dimethylaminobenzaldehyde or PDAB test) is the definitive identification test for Ergot alkaloids (which contain the indole nucleus of lysergic acid). When an extract of ergot is treated with Van Urk’s reagent in the presence of a trace amount of ferric chloride and sulfuric acid, a deep, distinct blue color develops, confirming the presence of these potent obstetric and anti-migraine alkaloids.

### Q18. Saponins are complex, naturally occurring plant glycosides. The simplest and most universal physical screening test utilized to detect the presence of Saponin glycosides in a crude drug extract is the:
A) Foam test
B) Shinoda test
C) Bornträger's test
D) Dragendorff’s test

**Correct Answer:** A) Foam test
**Explanation:** Saponins derive their name from the Latin word "sapo," meaning soap. Because their molecular structure contains both a lipophilic aglycone and a hydrophilic sugar chain, they act as powerful natural surfactants. The classic Foam Test involves shaking an aqueous extract of the plant vigorously in a test tube. If saponins are present, they produce a thick, stable, honeycomb-like froth that persists for at least 15 minutes. 

### Q19. True Cinnamon bark, highly prized for its aromatic volatile oils and culinary uses, is obtained from Cinnamomum zeylanicum. To which botanical family does this tree belong?
A) Myrtaceae
B) Umbelliferae
C) Lauraceae
D) Rubiaceae

**Correct Answer:** C) Lauraceae
**Explanation:** Cinnamomum zeylanicum (True Ceylon Cinnamon) belongs to the family Lauraceae. The dried inner bark contains a high concentration of volatile oil, whose primary active chemical constituent is Cinnamaldehyde (giving it its distinct flavor and odor) along with a small amount of Eugenol. Knowing the botanical families of major crude drugs is a staple requirement for competitive pharmacy exams. 

### Q20. Ashwagandha, frequently referred to as "Indian Ginseng," is a premier adaptogen in Ayurvedic medicine. What is the official biological source (botanical name) of this highly valued plant?
A) Glycyrrhiza glabra
B) Withania somnifera
C) Rauwolfia serpentina
D) Commiphora wightii

**Correct Answer:** B) Withania somnifera
**Explanation:** Ashwagandha is obtained from the dried roots of Withania somnifera, which belongs to the Solanaceae family. It contains complex steroidal lactones collectively known as Withanolides (such as Withaferin-A). It is heavily utilized for its neuroprotective, anxiolytic, and stamina-enhancing properties. Glycyrrhiza glabra is Liquorice, and Rauwolfia serpentina is Indian Snakeroot.

### Q21. Peppermint oil is a widely utilized aromatic carminative and flavoring agent. The primary active chemical constituent responsible for the distinct cooling sensation and pharmacological effect of this volatile oil is:
A) Eugenol
B) Cinnamaldehyde
C) Menthol
D) Thymol

**Correct Answer:** C) Menthol
**Explanation:** Peppermint oil is extracted via steam distillation from the fresh flowering tops of Mentha piperita (family Labiatae). Its most abundant and pharmacologically active constituent is Menthol (a monoterpene alcohol), which can crystallize out of the oil upon cooling. Menthol acts directly on cold-sensitive TRPM8 receptors in the skin and mucous membranes, triggering a pronounced cooling sensation, and it exerts mild local anesthetic and antispasmodic effects in the GI tract.

## Section 4: Jurisprudence, Biochemistry & Anatomy

### Q22. Under the Pharmacy Act of 1948, the State Pharmacy Council is responsible for maintaining the state register of pharmacists. According to the Act, how many members are elected to the State Council by the registered pharmacists of that state?
A) 3 members
B) 4 members
C) 5 members
D) 6 members

**Correct Answer:** D) 6 members
**Explanation:** The composition of the State Pharmacy Council is a frequent jurisprudence question. The Council consists of Elected, Nominated, and Ex-officio members. Exactly 6 members are elected from amongst themselves by the registered pharmacists residing in that specific state. In addition, one member is elected by the State Medical Council. The State Government also nominates 5 members, of whom at least 3 must possess a degree or diploma in pharmacy.

### Q23. According to the stringent rules under the Drugs and Cosmetics Act, which specific Schedule outlines the absolute minimum physical space, equipment, and reference books required to legally run a retail pharmacy?
A) Schedule M
B) Schedule N
C) Schedule P
D) Schedule O

**Correct Answer:** B) Schedule N
**Explanation:** Schedule N dictates the minimum requirements for the efficient and legal functioning of a pharmacy. It specifies the minimum floor area (e.g., 10 square meters), the required furniture, the necessity of a refrigerator for thermolabile drugs (like vaccines and insulin), and the mandatory presence of reference books (like the current edition of the Indian Pharmacopoeia and the Drugs and Cosmetics Act). 

### Q24. In pharmaceutical jurisprudence, if a drug is manufactured under a name which legally belongs to another drug, or if it is an exact imitation of another drug, the law strictly defines this specific product as a:
A) Spurious drug
B) Adulterated drug
C) Misbranded drug
D) Orphan drug

**Correct Answer:** A) Spurious drug
**Explanation:** The Drugs and Cosmetics Act draws very strict legal lines. A "Spurious Drug" is essentially a counterfeit or fake drug. It includes drugs manufactured under a name belonging to another drug, imitations, or drugs whose labels bear the name of a fictitious manufacturer. "Adulterated" means it contains filthy, putrid, or decomposed substances. "Misbranded" means it is colored or coated to conceal damage, or its label makes false claims.

### Q25. While the human body can synthesize many lipids, there are certain "Essential Fatty Acids" that cannot be synthesized endogenously and must be strictly obtained from the diet. Which of the following is an essential fatty acid?
A) Palmitic acid
B) Stearic acid
C) Oleic acid
D) Linoleic acid

**Correct Answer:** D) Linoleic acid
**Explanation:** The human body lacks the necessary desaturase enzymes to introduce double bonds beyond carbon 9 in the fatty acid chain. Therefore, Linoleic acid (an Omega-6) and Alpha-linolenic acid (an Omega-3) are strictly categorized as Essential Fatty Acids. They are crucial precursors for the biosynthesis of powerful local hormones known as eicosanoids (prostaglandins, thromboxanes, and leukotrienes), which regulate inflammation and immunity. Arachidonic acid becomes essential only if Linoleic acid is deficient.

### Q26. During the complex molecular process of Translation (protein synthesis), the ribosome reads the mRNA strand. Which of the following is universally recognized as a "Stop Codon" that completely terminates the growing polypeptide chain?
A) AUG
B) UAG
C) GCA
D) UUU

**Correct Answer:** B) UAG
**Explanation:** The genetic code contains exactly 64 codons. Only three of these are "Stop Codons" (also known as nonsense codons) which do not code for any amino acid. When the ribosome encounters UAA (Ochre), UAG (Amber), or UGA (Opal), a release factor binds to the ribosome, the translation process halts immediately, and the newly synthesized protein is released into the cell. AUG is the universal "Start Codon" (coding for Methionine).

### Q27. A patient presents with severe fissures at the corners of their mouth (Angular stomatitis), a swollen, magenta-colored tongue (Glossitis), and dermatitis. This clinical triad is the hallmark presentation of a severe deficiency in which specific vitamin?
A) Vitamin B1 (Thiamine)
B) Vitamin B2 (Riboflavin)
C) Vitamin B6 (Pyridoxine)
D) Vitamin B12 (Cyanocobalamin)

**Correct Answer:** B) Vitamin B2 (Riboflavin)
**Explanation:** Riboflavin (Vitamin B2) is a critical water-soluble vitamin acting as a precursor for the coenzymes FMN and FAD, which are essential for cellular redox reactions. A deficiency in Riboflavin classically presents as Ariboflavinosis. The hallmark, heavily tested symptoms include severe cracking at the corners of the mouth (Cheilosis/Angular stomatitis), a painful, swollen magenta tongue (Glossitis), and seborrheic dermatitis. 

### Q28. The human anatomical system is complex and highly specialized. Which of the following organs is definitively recognized as the absolute largest internal organ in the human body?
A) The Lungs
B) The Brain
C) The Heart
D) The Liver

**Correct Answer:** D) The Liver
**Explanation:** The Liver is the largest internal organ (and the largest gland) in the human body, weighing approximately 1.5 kilograms in a normal adult. Located in the right upper quadrant of the abdominal cavity, it performs over 500 vital functions, including massive detoxification (first-pass metabolism), glycogen storage, bile production, and the synthesis of almost all blood clotting factors and plasma proteins (like albumin). The skin is the largest organ overall.

### Q29. In a standard Complete Blood Count (CBC) laboratory report for a healthy, adult human, the normal physiological reference range for circulating Platelets (Thrombocytes) is:
A) 4,000 to 11,000 per microliter
B) 1.5 Lakhs to 4.5 Lakhs per microliter
C) 4.5 Million to 5.5 Million per microliter
D) 50,000 to 100,000 per microliter

**Correct Answer:** B) 1.5 Lakhs to 4.5 Lakhs per microliter
**Explanation:** Platelets are tiny cell fragments derived from massive megakaryocytes in the bone marrow, absolutely critical for primary hemostasis (blood clotting). The normal platelet count ranges from 150,000 to 450,000 per microliter (1.5 to 4.5 Lakhs). A dangerous drop below this (Thrombocytopenia), such as in severe Dengue fever, puts the patient at extreme risk of spontaneous, fatal hemorrhage. 4,000-11,000 is the normal WBC count, and 4.5-5.5 Million is the normal RBC count.

### Q30. The brain and spinal cord are physically protected and nourished by Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF). In a normal, healthy adult, the total circulating volume of CSF at any given time is approximately:
A) 50 mL
B) 150 mL
C) 500 mL
D) 1000 mL

**Correct Answer:** B) 150 mL
**Explanation:** Cerebrospinal Fluid (CSF) is continuously produced by the ependymal cells of the choroid plexus within the ventricles of the brain. While the body produces about 500 mL of CSF per day, it is constantly being reabsorbed into the venous circulation by the arachnoid villi. Therefore, the total volume present in the central nervous system at any one time is highly maintained at approximately 150 mL.

### 1. Zero-Order Elimination Kinetics Drugs
**Mnemonic: PEA** (Or **WATT POWER** for a larger list)
These drugs saturate liver enzymes quickly. A small dose increase leads to massive toxicity.
* **P**henytoin (Antiepileptic)
* **E**thanol (Alcohol)
* **A**spirin (At high, toxic doses)
*(Also: Warfarin, Theophylline, Tolbutamide)*

### 2. Protein Synthesis Inhibitors (Antibiotics)
**Mnemonic: "Buy AT 30, CELL at 50"**
Match the antibiotic class to the exact bacterial ribosomal subunit it attacks:
* **AT 30S:**
  * **A**minoglycosides (Gentamicin)
  * **T**etracyclines (Doxycycline)
* **CELL at 50S:**
  * **C**hloramphenicol
  * **E**rythromycin (Macrolides)
  * **L**incosamides (Clindamycin)
  * **L**inezolid

### 3. Essential Amino Acids
**Mnemonic: PVT TIM HALL** (Private Tim Hall)
These cannot be synthesized by humans and must be eaten.
* **P**henylalanine, **V**aline, **T**hreonine
* **T**ryptophan, **I**soleucine, **M**ethionine
* **H**istidine, **A**rginine, **L**eucine, **L**ysine
